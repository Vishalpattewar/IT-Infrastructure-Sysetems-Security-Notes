**Q1. A compliance audit is primarily conducted to:**

A) Identify and exploit vulnerabilities in IT systems
B) Verify whether an organization adheres to defined standards,
   laws, or regulations ✅
C) Test the physical security of an organization's premises
D) Develop new security policies for the organization

> **Explanation:** A compliance audit verifies **adherence** to
> defined rules — regulations, standards, or internal policies.
> It is NOT a penetration test (A), a physical security inspection
> (C), or a policy development exercise (D). The key word is
> "verify adherence."

---

**Q2. The phase of a security audit that involves collecting
evidence through interviews, document reviews, and observations
is called:**

A) Planning
B) Reporting
C) Follow-up
D) Fieldwork ✅

> **Explanation:** **Fieldwork** is where auditors collect evidence
> — interviews, document reviews, observations, and control testing.
> Planning defines scope. Reporting documents findings. Follow-up
> verifies corrective actions. Fieldwork is the hands-on evidence
> collection phase.

---

**Q3. The principle of auditing that requires auditors to be free
from conflicts of interest is called:**

A) Objectivity
B) Integrity
C) Independence ✅
D) Competence

> **Explanation:** **Independence** means the auditor has no
> conflict of interest with the auditee — they are free from
> bias or outside influence. Objectivity means conclusions are
> evidence-based. Integrity means ethical behavior. Competence
> means required skills. Independence is the structural
> requirement — objectivity is the mental state that results
> from independence.

---

**Q4. Common Criteria is published as which ISO standard?**

A) ISO/IEC 18045
B) ISO/IEC 19011
C) ISO/IEC 21827
D) ISO/IEC 15408 ✅

> **Explanation:** **Common Criteria = ISO/IEC 15408** — the
> international standard for IT product security evaluation.
> ISO/IEC 18045 = CEM (evaluation methodology). ISO/IEC 19011 =
> auditing management systems. ISO/IEC 21827 = SSE-CMM. These
> four ISO numbers are frequently tested together.

---

**Q5. In Common Criteria, EAL stands for:**

A) Evaluated Assurance Label
B) Evaluation Assurance Level ✅
C) External Audit Level
D) Evaluation Assessment List

> **Explanation:** **EAL = Evaluation Assurance Level** — the
> rating from 1 to 7 indicating how rigorously an IT product was
> evaluated. Higher EAL = more rigorous evaluation process —
> NOT more security features. EAL1 = lowest, EAL7 = highest.

---

**Q6. TCSEC is also known by which nickname?**

A) The Red Book
B) The Green Book
C) The Blue Book
D) The Orange Book ✅

> **Explanation:** **TCSEC = Orange Book** — named after its
> literal orange cover. It is the US DoD standard for trusted
> computer system evaluation. It is part of the Rainbow Series
> where each publication has a different colored cover. TCSEC
> was superseded by Common Criteria.

---

**Q7. NIST stands for and operates under which US government
department?**

A) National Information Security Technology — Dept of Defense
B) National Institute of Security Technology — Dept of Commerce
C) National Institute of Standards and Technology — Dept of
   Commerce ✅
D) National Institute of Standards and Technology — Dept of
   Homeland Security

> **Explanation:** **NIST = National Institute of Standards and
> Technology** — a US federal agency under the **Department of
> Commerce** (not Defense, not Homeland Security). NIST develops
> cybersecurity frameworks and guidelines including the CSF and
> RMF.

---

**Q8. The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) has how many main
components?**

A) Two — Core and Profiles
B) Three — Core, Tiers, and Profiles ✅
C) Four — Core, Tiers, Profiles, and Controls
D) Five — matching the five core functions

> **Explanation:** NIST CSF has exactly **three components**:
> **Framework Core** (WHAT — 5 functions), **Implementation Tiers**
> (HOW WELL — 4 levels), and **Framework Profiles** (WHERE —
> current vs target state). All three must be known together.

---

**Q9. Which NIST CSF function answers the question "How do we get
back to normal after an incident?"**

A) Respond
B) Detect
C) Protect
D) Recover ✅

> **Explanation:** **Recover** = restoring capabilities and services
> after a cybersecurity incident. Respond = taking action during an
> incident. Detect = identifying incidents. Protect = implementing
> safeguards to prevent incidents. Recover comes AFTER Respond.

---

**Q10. GDPR stands for and came into enforcement in which year?**

A) General Data Privacy Regulation — 2016
B) Global Data Protection Regulation — 2018
C) General Data Protection Regulation — 2018 ✅
D) General Data Protection Regulation — 2016

> **Explanation:** **GDPR = General Data Protection Regulation**
> — adopted in April 2016 but came into **enforcement on 25 May
> 2018** after a 2-year transition period. The regulation number
> is EU 2016/679. Confusing the adoption year (2016) with the
> enforcement year (2018) is a classic MCQ trap.

---

**Q11. How many core principles does GDPR Article 5 define for
processing personal data?**

A) 5
B) 6
C) 8
D) 7 ✅

> **Explanation:** GDPR Article 5 defines exactly **7 core
> principles**: Lawfulness/Fairness/Transparency, Purpose
> Limitation, Data Minimisation, Accuracy, Storage Limitation,
> Integrity and Confidentiality, and Accountability. The number
> 7 is directly and frequently tested.

---

**Q12. Under GDPR, the maximum fine for the most serious violations
(upper tier) is:**

A) €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover
B) €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover ✅
C) €50 million or 4% of global annual turnover
D) €20 million or 2% of global annual turnover

> **Explanation:** **Upper tier = €20 million OR 4% of global
> annual turnover** — whichever is HIGHER. This applies to
> violations of core principles, data subject rights, and
> international transfer rules. The lower tier is €10M/2%
> for less serious procedural violations.

---

**Q13. ISO/IEC 27001 was first published as an ISO standard in
which year?**

A) 2000
B) 2002
C) 2005 ✅
D) 2007

> **Explanation:** **ISO/IEC 27001:2005** was the first published
> ISO version — replacing BS 7799 Part 2. The evolution is:
> BS 7799 Part 2 (1998/2002) → ISO/IEC 27001:2005 → 2013 → 2022.
> The year 2000 relates to ISO 17799 (the code of practice) not
> the certifiable ISMS standard.

---

**Q14. ISO/IEC 27001:2022 organized Annex A controls into how
many themes and how many controls?**

A) 14 domains — 114 controls
B) 11 domains — 133 controls
C) 5 themes — 100 controls
D) 4 themes — 93 controls ✅

> **Explanation:** **ISO 27001:2022 = 4 themes, 93 controls** —
> the current version. The four themes are Organizational (37),
> People (8), Physical (14), and Technological (34). The 2013
> version had 14 domains and 114 controls. The 2005 version had
> 11 domains and 133 controls.

---

**Q15. Which organization publishes ISO/IEC 27001 and is the
only body that can certify organizations to this standard?**

A) ISO publishes it and certifies organizations directly
B) ISO publishes it — accredited third-party Certification Bodies
   certify organizations ✅
C) NIST publishes it — ISO certifies organizations
D) ISACA publishes it — accredited bodies certify organizations

> **Explanation:** **ISO publishes the standard but does NOT
> certify organizations** — this is one of the most common MCQ
> traps. Accredited third-party **Certification Bodies** conduct
> Stage 1 and Stage 2 audits and issue certificates. ISO only
> writes and maintains the standard document.

---

**Q16. SOX stands for and was passed in response to which
corporate scandal?**

A) Securities Oversight Exchange Act — WorldCom only
B) Sarbanes-Oxley Act — Enron and WorldCom scandals ✅
C) Security Operations Exchange Act — Enron scandal only
D) Sarbanes-Oxley Act — Lehman Brothers collapse

> **Explanation:** **SOX = Sarbanes-Oxley Act** — named after
> Senator Paul Sarbanes and Representative Michael Oxley. It was
> passed in 2002 directly in response to the **Enron and WorldCom**
> accounting scandals that destroyed investor trust. Lehman Brothers
> collapsed in 2008 — a separate financial crisis event.

---

**Q17. SOC 2 reports are based on which framework's criteria?**

A) COSO Internal Control Framework
B) ISO/IEC 27001 control domains
C) AICPA Trust Services Criteria ✅
D) NIST SP 800-53 control families

> **Explanation:** **SOC 2 is based on AICPA's Trust Services
> Criteria (TSC)** — the 5 criteria being Security, Availability,
> Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, and Privacy. Security
> is the only mandatory one. COSO is used for SOX 404 ICFR
> assessment. ISO 27001 and NIST 800-53 are separate frameworks.

---

**Q18. COBIT is developed and maintained by which organization?**

A) ISO/IEC
B) ISACA ✅
C) AICPA
D) NIST

> **Explanation:** **COBIT is developed by ISACA (Information
> Systems Audit and Control Association)**. ISO/IEC develops
> international standards like ISO 27001. AICPA created the SOC
> reporting framework. NIST develops US government frameworks.
> Knowing ISACA = COBIT is a basic fact tested in every exam.

---

**Q19. COBIT vs ITIL — which statement is correct?**

A) COBIT = IT service management / ITIL = IT governance
B) COBIT = IT governance / ITIL = IT service management ✅
C) Both COBIT and ITIL focus on IT governance
D) COBIT is developed by AXELOS / ITIL is developed by ISACA

> **Explanation:** **COBIT = IT governance** (strategic —
> "are we doing the right things?") developed by **ISACA**.
> **ITIL = IT service management** (operational — "are we doing
> things right?") developed by **AXELOS**. They are complementary
> not competing. Developer organization is also reversed in the
> trap option D.

---

**Q20. HIPAA stands for and was enacted in which year?**

A) Health Information Privacy and Accountability Act — 2003
B) Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act — 1996 ✅
C) Health Insurance Privacy and Accessibility Act — 1996
D) Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act — 2009

> **Explanation:** **HIPAA = Health Insurance Portability and
> Accountability Act** — enacted **21 August 1996** signed by
> President Bill Clinton. 2003 = when the Privacy Rule became
> effective. 2009 = when HITECH Act was enacted. The act itself
> is 1996.

---

**Q21. The HIPAA Security Rule applies to which form of PHI?**

A) All forms of PHI including paper, oral, and electronic records
B) Only paper-based medical records
C) Electronic PHI (ePHI) only ✅
D) Oral communications containing health information only

> **Explanation:** The **HIPAA Security Rule applies ONLY to
> ePHI (Electronic Protected Health Information)**. Paper records
> and oral communications are covered by the Privacy Rule —
> NOT the Security Rule. An organization can violate HIPAA
> through improper handling of paper records — the Privacy Rule
> covers that.

---

**Q22. Under HIPAA, "Addressable" security specifications mean:**

A) The specification is completely optional and can be skipped
B) Must be assessed — implemented if reasonable or documented
   with an alternative ✅
C) Must always be implemented exactly as written — no exceptions
D) Only applies to organizations with more than 500 employees

> **Explanation:** **Addressable ≠ Optional** — one of the most
> tested HIPAA concepts. Addressable specifications must be
> **assessed** — if reasonable and appropriate → implement. If
> not → **document why** and implement an equivalent alternative.
> Organizations cannot simply ignore them. Required specifications
> must always be implemented with no exceptions.

---

**Q23. PCI DSS is governed by which organization?**

A) ISACA — Information Systems Audit and Control Association
B) AICPA — American Institute of Certified Public Accountants
C) NIST — National Institute of Standards and Technology
D) PCI SSC — Payment Card Industry Security Standards Council ✅

> **Explanation:** **PCI DSS is governed by PCI SSC (Payment Card
> Industry Security Standards Council)** — founded in 2006 by
> Visa, Mastercard, American Express, Discover, and JCB. ISACA
> governs COBIT. AICPA governs SOC reports. NIST develops US
> government frameworks. PCI SSC is a private industry body.

---

**Q24. Under PCI DSS, Sensitive Authentication Data (SAD) such
as CVV2 codes may be stored:**

A) If encrypted with AES-256 encryption
B) Only by Level 1 merchants with QSA approval
C) For up to 24 hours after the transaction is authorized
D) Never — not under any circumstances after authorization ✅

> **Explanation:** **SAD cannot be stored after authorization —
> EVER — not even encrypted.** This is one of PCI DSS's most
> absolute rules. CVV2, CVC2, CID, full magnetic stripe data,
> and PINs fall into this category. No level of encryption or
> security makes SAD storage acceptable after a transaction
> is authorized.

---

**Q25. PCI DSS Merchant Level 1 applies to merchants processing
more than how many card transactions per year?**

A) 1 million transactions
B) 300,000 transactions
C) 6 million transactions ✅
D) 20,000 transactions

> **Explanation:** **Merchant Level 1 = more than 6 million
> Visa/Mastercard transactions per year** — the highest volume
> and strictest requirement tier. Level 1 merchants must have
> an annual ROC conducted by an external QSA. 1 million = Level
> 2 threshold. 300,000 = Service Provider Level 1 threshold.
> 20,000 = Level 3/4 e-commerce threshold.

---

**Q26. CIS stands for and was founded in which year?**

A) Cyber Intelligence Service — founded 2006
B) Center for Information Security — founded 1995
C) Center for Internet Security — founded 2000 ✅
D) Cyber Infrastructure Security — founded 2002

> **Explanation:** **CIS = Center for Internet Security** —
> a nonprofit organization founded in **2000** headquartered in
> East Greenbush, New York. 2006 = founding year of PCI SSC —
> a common confusion trap. CIS develops CIS Controls and CIS
> Benchmarks — both free to use.

---

**Q27. CIS Controls v8 published in 2021 contains how many
controls?**

A) 20 controls
B) 15 controls
C) 18 controls ✅
D) 22 controls

> **Explanation:** **CIS Controls v8 (May 2021) = 18 controls**
> — reduced from 20 in v7.1 (2019) through merging of related
> controls. No security requirements were removed — they were
> reorganized. 22 = total Process Areas in SSE-CMM — a different
> framework entirely.

---

**Q28. CIS Implementation Group 1 (IG1) is described as:**

A) Advanced security for large enterprises with expert teams
B) Essential cyber hygiene — the minimum baseline for ALL
   organizations ✅
C) Security controls for medium organizations with dedicated IT
D) Full implementation of all 18 CIS controls

> **Explanation:** **IG1 = Essential Cyber Hygiene** — targeted
> at small organizations with limited resources and representing
> the **minimum security baseline ALL organizations should
> implement** regardless of size. IG1 contains 56 safeguards.
> IG2 = medium organizations (130 safeguards). IG3 = large
> organizations (153 safeguards — full implementation).

---

**Q29. SSE-CMM stands for and was adopted as which ISO standard?**

A) Systems Software Engineering CMM — ISO/IEC 15408
B) Secure Systems Engineering CMM — ISO/IEC 27001
C) Systems Security Engineering Capability Maturity Model —
   ISO/IEC 21827 ✅
D) Systems Security Evaluation CMM — ISO/IEC 18045

> **Explanation:** **SSE-CMM = Systems Security Engineering
> Capability Maturity Model** — adopted as **ISO/IEC 21827**.
> ISO/IEC 15408 = Common Criteria. ISO/IEC 27001 = ISMS standard.
> ISO/IEC 18045 = CEM (evaluation methodology). The four ISO
> numbers (15408, 18045, 21827, 27001) are frequently tested
> together.

---

**Q30. SSE-CMM Capability Level 3 is named:**

A) Planned and Tracked
B) Quantitatively Controlled
C) Performed Informally
D) Well Defined ✅

> **Explanation:** **Level 3 = Well Defined** — a standard
> organizational process is defined, documented, and used
> consistently across all projects. Level 1 = Performed
> Informally. Level 2 = Planned and Tracked. Level 4 =
> Quantitatively Controlled. Level 5 = Continuously Improving.

---

**Q31. SSE-CMM has how many total Process Areas divided into
how many categories?**

A) 20 PAs — 2 categories (Security and Project)
B) 22 PAs — 3 categories (Security, Project, Organization) ✅
C) 11 PAs — 1 category (Security only)
D) 25 PAs — 3 categories

> **Explanation:** SSE-CMM has **22 Process Areas** in **3
> categories**: 11 Security PAs (PA01–PA11), 6 Project PAs
> (PA12–PA17), and 5 Organization PAs (PA18–PA22). The 11
> Security PAs are the core — covering what security engineering
> organizations must do.

---

**Q32. ITAA 2008 is best described as:**

A) India's first standalone cybersecurity law
B) A replacement for India's IT Act 2000
C) An amendment to India's IT Act 2000 ✅
D) A data protection regulation issued by MeitY

> **Explanation:** **ITAA 2008 = IT (Amendment) Act 2008** —
> it **amends** the original IT Act 2000, it does NOT replace it.
> The combined law is referred to as IT Act 2000 as amended by
> ITAA 2008. India's first comprehensive standalone data
> protection law is the DPDPA 2023 — a completely separate
> legislation.

---

**Q33. Section 66F of ITAA 2008 covers which offense?**

A) Identity theft — fraudulent use of passwords
B) Publishing obscene material online
C) Violation of privacy — capturing private images
D) Cyber terrorism — up to life imprisonment ✅

> **Explanation:** **Section 66F = Cyber Terrorism** — using
> computer resources to threaten national security, sovereignty,
> or damage critical infrastructure — punishable by **up to life
> imprisonment**. Section 66C = identity theft. Section 67 =
> obscene material. Section 66E = violation of privacy. Section
> 66F carries the harshest punishment in ITAA 2008.

---

**Q34. DPDPA 2023 received Presidential assent on which date?**

A) 5 February 2023
B) 27 October 2023
C) 25 May 2023
D) 11 August 2023 ✅

> **Explanation:** **DPDPA 2023 received Presidential assent on
> 11 August 2023** and was published in the Official Gazette on
> the same date. Administered by MeitY and enforced through the
> Data Protection Board of India (DPBI). This date is one of
> the most directly tested facts from Session 12.

---

**Q35. Under DPDPA 2023, the term "Data Fiduciary" corresponds
to which GDPR term?**

A) Data Subject
B) Data Processor
C) Data Protection Officer
D) Data Controller ✅

> **Explanation:** **Data Fiduciary (DPDPA) = Data Controller
> (GDPR)** — the entity that determines the purpose and means
> of processing personal data. The full mapping:
> Data Principal = Data Subject.
> Data Fiduciary = Data Controller.
> Data Processor = Data Processor (same term).
> DPBI = DPA (national supervisory authority).

**Q36. The maximum financial penalty under DPDPA 2023 is:**

A) ₹100 crore per violation
B) ₹200 crore per violation
C) ₹250 crore per violation ✅
D) ₹500 crore per violation

> **Explanation:** **Maximum DPDPA penalty = ₹250 crore** —
> applicable to failure to implement security safeguards that
> results in a data breach. Other penalties: ₹200 crore for
> children's data violations and failure to notify breaches,
> ₹150 crore for SDF obligation failures, ₹50 crore for DPBI
> order non-compliance.

**Q37. The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) was enacted by
which country and in which year?**

A) UK — 2010
B) USA — 1977 ✅
C) USA — 2010
D) UK — 1977

> **Explanation:** **FCPA = USA, enacted 1977** — covering
> the bribery of foreign government officials by US companies
> and persons. The **UK Bribery Act (UKBA) = UK, enacted 2010**
> — covering all bribery including commercial. The year and
> country pairing is a classic MCQ trap — FCPA is 1977 USA,
> UKBA is 2010 UK.

---

**Q38. A key difference between FCPA and UKBA is that UKBA
covers which additional type of bribery that FCPA does not?**

A) Bribery of domestic government officials
B) Bribery of international organization officials
C) Commercial bribery between private parties ✅
D) Bribery involving cryptocurrency transactions

> **Explanation:** **UKBA covers ALL bribery including commercial
> bribery** between private parties — not just government official
> bribery. **FCPA covers only foreign government official bribery**
> — commercial bribery between private businesses is outside FCPA
> scope. This broader scope is what makes UKBA stricter than FCPA.

---

**Q39. ISO 37001 is the international standard for which
management system?**

A) Information security management systems
B) Environmental management systems
C) Quality management systems
D) Anti-bribery management systems ✅

> **Explanation:** **ISO 37001 = Anti-Bribery Management Systems**
> — it provides a framework to prevent, detect, and address
> bribery. Under the UK Bribery Act, having "adequate procedures"
> equivalent to ISO 37001 is a valid corporate defense. ISO 27001
> = information security. ISO 14001 = environmental. ISO 9001 =
> quality.

**Q40. In the Global Bank internal audit case study, which
framework was used as the foundation for assessing Internal
Controls over Financial Reporting (ICFR) for SOX compliance?**

A) COBIT — IT governance framework
B) ISO 27001 — information security management
C) NIST CSF — cybersecurity risk management
D) COSO — Committee of Sponsoring Organizations ✅

> **Explanation:** **COSO is the primary framework for SOX 404
> ICFR assessment** — management uses COSO to assess and
> document internal controls over financial reporting. COBIT was
> also used — but specifically for IT governance and IT audit
> methodology within the SOX program. COSO covers the broader
> internal control assessment that SOX 404 requires.
