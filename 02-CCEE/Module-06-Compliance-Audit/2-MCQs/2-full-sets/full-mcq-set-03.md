## ✅ Answers and Explanations

---

**Q1. Correct Answer: D — Audit Charter**

> The **Audit Charter** is the formal document that defines the
> internal audit function's purpose, authority, and responsibility.
> It is approved by the **Board or Audit Committee** — giving
> auditors formal authority to access systems, records, and personnel.
> Without a charter, auditors have no formal authority. An Audit
> Report documents findings. An Audit Plan covers a specific
> audit engagement. Scope Statement defines boundaries.

---

**Q2. Correct Answer: C — Major Nonconformity**

> **Major Nonconformity** = a serious, systemic failure where a
> required control is **completely absent** or a process completely
> fails to meet requirements. **Minor Nonconformity** = an isolated
> or partial gap where the control exists but has small failures.
> Observation = potential future risk — not a current failure.
> OFI = optional suggestion only — not a failure at all.

---

**Q3. Correct Answer: D — First Party Audit**

> **First Party Audit** = the organization audits itself — conducted
> by its own internal audit team. Second Party = a customer auditing
> its supplier. Third Party = an independent external body (like a
> certification body or regulator). External Audit is a general term
> for Third Party — but First Party is the precise ISO 19011
> classification for self-auditing.

---

**Q4. Correct Answer: B — France, Germany, Netherlands, UK**

> **ITSEC was jointly developed by France, Germany, Netherlands,
> and the United Kingdom** — Europe's answer to the US TCSEC
> standard. It covered confidentiality, integrity, AND availability
> — broader than TCSEC which focused only on confidentiality.
> ITSEC used E0–E6 assurance levels and was later superseded
> by Common Criteria.

---

**Q5. Correct Answer: D — Formally Verified Design and Tested**

> **EAL7 = Formally Verified Design and Tested** — the highest
> assurance level requiring complete **formal mathematical proof**
> of the design and implementation. Used for extreme security
> environments such as military and classified government systems.
> EAL4 = Methodically Designed, Tested and Reviewed (most
> commercial). EAL6 = Semiformally Verified. EAL1 = Functionally
> Tested (lowest).

---

**Q6. Correct Answer: C — ISO/IEC 18045**

> **CEM = Common Evaluation Methodology = ISO/IEC 18045** —
> defines HOW evaluators must conduct CC evaluations. ISO/IEC
> 15408 = Common Criteria (WHAT to evaluate). ISO/IEC 21827 =
> SSE-CMM. ISO/IEC 19011 = auditing management systems.
> CC (15408) defines the what — CEM (18045) defines the how —
> they work together as companion standards.

---

**Q7. Correct Answer: C — Govern**

> **NIST CSF 2.0 (February 2024) added the GOVERN function** as
> the 6th function — making the framework G-IPDRR instead of
> just IPDRR. Govern is an overarching function providing context
> and direction for all other five functions — covering
> organizational context, risk strategy, policies, roles, and
> supply chain risk management. Authorize is an RMF step —
> not a CSF function.

---

**Q8. Correct Answer: D — Categorize**

> **NIST RMF Step 2 = Categorize** — classifying the information
> system based on potential impact (Low, Moderate, High) using
> FIPS 199. Step 1 = Prepare. Step 3 = Select (choose controls
> from SP 800-53). Step 5 = Assess (verify controls work). The
> 7 RMF steps are: Prepare → Categorize → Select → Implement →
> Assess → Authorize → Monitor.

---

**Q9. Correct Answer: D — Partial**

> **Tier 1 = Partial** — characterized by ad hoc, reactive
> security practices with no formal risk management process.
> The four tiers are: Partial (1) → Risk Informed (2) →
> Repeatable (3) → Adaptive (4). Tier 4 = Adaptive (highest).
> Tiers are NOT maturity levels — the right tier depends on
> an organization's risk tolerance and business needs.

---

**Q10. Correct Answer: C — It applies directly and uniformly
across all EU member states without national legislation**

> A **Regulation** (like GDPR) applies **directly and uniformly**
> across all EU member states without each country needing to
> pass its own implementing law. A **Directive** (like the old
> 95/46/EC) required each country to pass national legislation.
> GDPR applies to both public and private sector organizations —
> not just public sector. It also applies to non-EU organizations
> through its extraterritorial reach.

---

**Q11. Correct Answer: D — Right to Erasure**

> The **Right to Erasure = Right to be Forgotten** — Article 17
> of GDPR. It allows individuals to request deletion of their
> personal data. It is NOT absolute — exceptions exist for legal
> obligations, public interest, and freedom of expression. Right
> to Restrict Processing = pause but don't delete. Right to Object
> = stop processing for direct marketing. Right to Data Portability
> = receive data in machine-readable format.

---

**Q12. Correct Answer: D — Data Controller**

> The **Data Controller** decides WHY (purpose) and HOW (means)
> personal data is processed and bears **primary GDPR compliance
> responsibility**. The Data Processor processes data on behalf
> of the controller as instructed. The DPO monitors compliance
> but does not control processing. The Data Subject is the
> individual whose data is being processed.

---

**Q13. Correct Answer: C — BS 7799**

> **BS 7799** published by the British Standards Institution (BSI)
> is the direct predecessor of ISO 27001. BS 7799 Part 1 became
> ISO 17799 (later renumbered ISO 27002). BS 7799 Part 2 became
> ISO 27001. BS 7700 and BS 7800 are unrelated British standards.
> BS 8800 relates to occupational health and safety — not
> information security.

---

**Q14. Correct Answer: D — 11 domains — 133 controls**

> **ISO 27001:2005 = 11 domains, 133 controls** — the first ISO
> version. The three version facts to memorize: 2005 (11 domains,
> 133 controls) → 2013 (14 domains, 114 controls) → 2022 (4
> themes, 93 controls). Option A = 2013 version facts. Option B =
> 2022 version facts. Option C is fictional — never existed.

---

**Q15. Correct Answer: B — Do**

> **Clause 8 (Operation) = DO phase** of PDCA — implementing and
> operating the ISMS, deploying the controls selected in planning.
> The PDCA mapping: Plan = Clauses 4, 5, 6, 7 (establish ISMS).
> Do = Clause 8 (implement ISMS). Check = Clause 9 (monitor and
> review). Act = Clause 10 (improve and correct nonconformities).

---

**Q16. Correct Answer: C — SOX itself — specifically to oversee
auditors of public companies**

> **PCAOB was created by SOX Section 101** in 2002 — it is a
> direct product of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act. Before SOX, auditors
> of public companies were self-regulated through the AICPA.
> SOX created PCAOB to provide independent oversight of public
> company auditors — removing self-regulation. PCAOB reports to
> the SEC.

---

**Q17. Correct Answer: D — SOC 3**

> **SOC 3** is the only publicly distributable SOC report —
> it contains the same scope as SOC 2 but with significantly
> less detail, making it appropriate for general public consumption.
> Organizations can display an AICPA SOC 3 seal on their website.
> SOC 1 and SOC 2 are restricted reports — typically shared only
> under NDA with customers and regulators.

---

**Q18. Correct Answer: B — 4 domains — 34 processes**

> **COBIT 4.1 = 4 domains, 34 processes** — the four domains being
> Plan and Organise (PO), Acquire and Implement (AI), Deliver and
> Support (DS), and Monitor and Evaluate (ME). It also had 210
> control objectives. COBIT 5 = 5 domains, 37 processes. COBIT
> 2019 = 5 domains, 40 objectives.

---

**Q19. Correct Answer: D — 5**

> **COBIT 5 has 5 principles**: Meeting Stakeholder Needs,
> Covering the Enterprise End-to-End, Applying a Single
> Integrated Framework, Enabling a Holistic Approach, and
> Separating Governance from Management. **COBIT 2019 updated
> this to 6 principles** — adding Dynamic Governance System and
> Tailored to Enterprise Needs. The 5 vs 6 distinction is
> directly tested.

---

**Q20. Correct Answer: C — Stakeholder Needs**

> The **Goals Cascade** starts at the top with **Stakeholder
> Needs** — then flows down to Enterprise Goals (17 goals across
> 4 BSC perspectives) → Alignment Goals (13 IT-related goals) →
> Governance and Management Objectives (40 in COBIT 2019).
> The cascade justifies every IT governance activity in business
> terms — connecting what the board cares about to what IT does.

---

**Q21. Correct Answer: C — 2009 — American Recovery and
Reinvestment Act (ARRA)**

> **HITECH = Health Information Technology for Economic and
> Clinical Health Act** — enacted as part of the **American
> Recovery and Reinvestment Act (ARRA) on February 17, 2009.**
> HITECH significantly strengthened HIPAA by adding breach
> notification, raising penalties, extending obligations to
> Business Associates, and promoting Electronic Health Record
> (EHR) adoption.

---

**Q22. Correct Answer: C — 2003**

> The **HIPAA Privacy Rule became effective April 14, 2003** —
> after a transition period following HIPAA's enactment in 1996.
> The Security Rule became effective April 21, 2005. HIPAA itself
> was enacted in 1996. The HITECH Act came in 2009. These dates
> are frequently tested together — 1996 (enacted), 2003 (Privacy
> Rule), 2005 (Security Rule), 2009 (HITECH).

---

**Q23. Correct Answer: C — Administrative, Physical, and
Technical Safeguards**

> The HIPAA Security Rule requires exactly **three categories of
> safeguards: Administrative, Physical, and Technical.**
> Administrative = policies and management processes.
> Physical = physical access controls for ePHI systems.
> Technical = technology controls like encryption and access
> controls. Each category contains a mix of Required (mandatory)
> and Addressable (assess and implement or document) specifications.

---

**Q24. Correct Answer: C — 2004**

> **PCI DSS 1.0 was published in 2004** — the first unified
> standard combining Visa's CISP and Mastercard's SDP programs.
> The PCI SSC (Security Standards Council) was then founded in
> 2006 to take ownership of the standard. The year 2000 marks
> Visa's CISP launch — the precursor program. PCI DSS 1.1 was
> released in 2006 alongside PCI SSC's founding.

---

**Q25. Correct Answer: B — Visa, Mastercard, American Express,
Discover, JCB**

> The **5 founding members of PCI SSC** are Visa, Mastercard,
> American Express, Discover, and **JCB (Japan Credit Bureau)** —
> the only non-US founding member. UnionPay (Chinese card brand)
> is NOT a founding member. PayPal is not a founding member.
> Diners Club is not a founding member. JCB is the commonly
> missed founding member.

---

**Q26. Correct Answer: C — Implementing network segmentation
to isolate the CDE**

> **Network segmentation** is the most effective scope reduction
> technique — isolating the Cardholder Data Environment (CDE)
> from other systems means those other systems are out of PCI DSS
> scope. Encryption protects data but does NOT remove systems
> from scope. Using an SAQ vs ROC is about validation method —
> not scope reduction. Storing only last 4 digits removes data
> but the system is still in scope.

---

**Q27. Correct Answer: D — 20**

> **CIS Controls v7.1 (2019) had 20 controls** — this was
> reduced to 18 in v8 (2021) through merging of related controls.
> No security requirements were eliminated — they were reorganized.
> 22 = total Process Areas in SSE-CMM. 15 is not a recognized
> version count. The reduction from 20 to 18 is one of the most
> tested CIS version facts.

---

**Q28. Correct Answer: C — 153**

> **IG3 = 153 total safeguards** — the full implementation of
> all 18 CIS controls. Since IGs are cumulative: IG1 = 56
> safeguards (baseline). IG2 = 56 + 74 = 130 total. IG3 = 130
> + 23 = 153 total. 171 was the total sub-controls in v7.1 —
> not v8. An IG3 organization implements every safeguard across
> all 18 controls.

---

**Q29. Correct Answer: B — Level 1 (Performed Informally)
represents the starting point — even informal performance
of Base Practices qualifies as Level 1**

> **SSE-CMM starts at Level 1 — not Level 0** because even
> informal, ad hoc performance of Base Practices is recognized
> as a starting capability level. Level 0 does not exist in
> SSE-CMM. This differs from COBIT 4.1's maturity model which
> starts at Level 0 (Non-Existent). The SSE-CMM philosophy
> recognizes that any security engineering activity — even
> informal — has value.

---

**Q30. Correct Answer: D — Administering security controls**

> **PA01 = Administer Security Controls** — manage and administer
> security mechanisms and controls. PA03 = Assess Security Risk.
> PA10 = Specify Security Needs. PA11 = Verify and Validate
> Security. The 11 Security PAs run from PA01 to PA11 and cover
> the full range of security engineering activities from
> specifying needs to monitoring security posture.

---

**Q31. Correct Answer: C — Common Criteria evaluates products —
SSE-CMM evaluates organizations' security engineering processes**

> **Common Criteria** evaluates the security of IT **products**
> (the Target of Evaluation — TOE) and produces an EAL rating.
> **SSE-CMM** evaluates the **security engineering process
> capability** of **organizations** — how mature and capable
> are their processes for building secure systems? They are
> complementary — a product can be CC-evaluated AND the
> organization that built it can be SSE-CMM appraised.

---

**Q32. Correct Answer: D — Identity theft — fraudulent use of
passwords or electronic signatures**

> **Section 66C = Identity Theft** — fraudulently or dishonestly
> making use of another person's electronic signature, password,
> or any other unique identification feature. Punishment: up to
> 3 years + fine up to ₹1 lakh. Section 66F = cyber terrorism
> (life imprisonment). Section 66E = violation of privacy.
> Section 67 = obscene material.

---

**Q33. Correct Answer: C — Safe harbor — exemption from liability
with due diligence and compliance with government directions**

> **Section 79 = Intermediary Safe Harbor** — internet
> intermediaries (social media, hosting providers, search
> engines) are NOT liable for third-party content if they
> exercise due diligence, comply with government notices, and
> follow IT (Intermediary Guidelines) Rules. This is India's
> equivalent of the US Section 230 safe harbor. Section 69
> covers government interception powers.

---

**Q34. Correct Answer: D — Data Subject**

> **Data Principal (DPDPA) = Data Subject (GDPR)** — the
> individual whose personal data is being processed. The
> complete DPDPA to GDPR terminology mapping:
> Data Principal = Data Subject.
> Data Fiduciary = Data Controller.
> Data Processor = Data Processor (same term).
> DPBI = DPA (national supervisory authority).
> Consent Manager = unique DPDPA concept with no GDPR equivalent.

---

**Q35. Correct Answer: C — 18**

> **DPDPA 2023 requires parental consent for children under 18**
> — a higher threshold than GDPR which sets the age at under 16
> (with EU member states able to lower it to 13). This means more
> individuals require parental consent in India than under GDPR.
> DPDPA's higher children's age threshold is one of its more
> conservative provisions compared to GDPR.

---

**Q36. Correct Answer: B — A statutory adjudicatory body that
adjudicates complaints and imposes penalties**

> **DPBI = Data Protection Board of India** — a statutory body
> that adjudicates complaints from Data Principals, investigates
> DPDPA violations, and imposes financial penalties. It functions
> as a **digital office** — proceedings conducted through digital
> means. DPBI decisions are appealable to the High Court. It
> is NOT a ministry, NOT a certification body, and NOT merely
> an advisory committee.

---

**Q37. Correct Answer: D — A blocklist — all countries permitted
except those on a government-restricted list**

> **DPDPA uses a blocklist approach** — data can be transferred
> to ANY country EXCEPT those on a government-issued restricted
> list. **GDPR uses an allowlist approach** — transfers only
> permitted to countries with an adequacy decision or using
> SCCs/BCRs. DPDPA's approach is more business-friendly and
> less restrictive than GDPR's default-restrict model.

---

**Q38. Correct Answer: C — Foreign government officials**

> **FCPA prohibits bribing foreign government officials** —
> it does NOT cover commercial bribery between private parties.
> The UKBA (UK Bribery Act 2010) covers ALL bribery including
> commercial. This is the most fundamental difference between
> FCPA and UKBA — FCPA = government officials only, UKBA =
> government officials AND commercial bribery.

---

**Q39. Correct Answer: C — Justice BN Srikrishna Committee**

> The **Justice BN Srikrishna Committee** (formally the Expert
> Committee on Data Protection) published its comprehensive 2018
> report recommending India's first data protection law — the
> Personal Data Protection Bill (PDPB). This eventually led to
> the DPDPA 2023 after multiple revisions. The report was heavily
> influenced by GDPR principles and took over 5 years to become
> law.

---

**Q40. Correct Answer: B — Adequate procedures — equivalent to
ISO 37001 compliance**

> Under the **UK Bribery Act Section 7**, a commercial organization
> can use **"adequate procedures"** as a defense against prosecution
> for failure to prevent bribery. Having procedures equivalent to
> **ISO 37001 (Anti-Bribery Management Systems)** is recognized
> as demonstrating adequate procedures. This defense does NOT
> apply if the bribery was committed by a senior officer of the
> organization itself.
