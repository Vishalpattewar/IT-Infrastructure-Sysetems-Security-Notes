**Q1. ITAA 2008 is best described as:**

A) A completely new Indian cyber law that replaced the IT Act 2000
B) An amendment to India's Information Technology Act 2000 that
   expanded cybercrime and data protection provisions ✅
C) India's first comprehensive personal data protection law
D) A regulation issued by MeitY to govern e-commerce transactions

> **Explanation:** **ITAA 2008 = Information Technology (Amendment)
> Act 2008** — it is an **amendment** to the original IT Act 2000,
> NOT a replacement. The combined law is referred to as the IT Act
> 2000 as amended by ITAA 2008. ITAA 2008 added new cybercrime
> provisions, Section 43A for data protection, and formally
> established CERT-In. India's first comprehensive data protection
> law is the DPDPA 2023 — a separate act.

---

**Q2. ITAA 2008 received Presidential assent and became effective
on which dates respectively?**

A) 27 October 2008 — effective 5 February 2009
B) 5 February 2009 — effective 27 October 2009 ✅
C) 11 August 2008 — effective 1 January 2009
D) 27 October 2009 — effective 5 February 2010

> **Explanation:** **ITAA 2008 received Presidential assent on
> 5 February 2009** and became **effective from 27 October 2009**.
> The gap between assent and effective date allowed organizations
> time to prepare for compliance. The ministry administering ITAA
> 2008 is MeitY (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology).

---

**Q3. Section 43A of ITAA 2008 introduced which significant
provision for the first time in Indian law?**

A) Criminal punishment for cyber terrorism
B) Government power to block websites
C) Formal establishment of CERT-In as national agency
D) Liability for body corporates that fail to protect sensitive
   personal data or information (SPDI) ✅

> **Explanation:** **Section 43A** introduced India's **first data
> protection provision** — making body corporates liable for
> compensation if they negligently fail to maintain reasonable
> security practices for **Sensitive Personal Data or Information
> (SPDI)**. Section 66F covers cyber terrorism. Section 69A covers
> website blocking. Section 70B establishes CERT-In. Section 43A
> was the only data protection provision for 14 years until DPDPA
> 2023.

---

**Q4. Under Section 43A of ITAA 2008, which security standard is
specifically cited as an acceptable standard for "reasonable
security practices"?**

A) NIST SP 800-53
B) PCI DSS v4.0
C) ISO/IEC 27001 ✅
D) CIS Controls v8

> **Explanation:** The IT (Reasonable Security Practices and
> Procedures) Rules 2011 — issued under Section 43A — specifically
> cite **ISO/IEC 27001** as an acceptable standard for demonstrating
> reasonable security practices. This makes ISO 27001 certification
> directly relevant to ITAA 2008 compliance for body corporates
> handling SPDI in India.

---

**Q5. Section 66F of ITAA 2008 covers which offense and carries
what maximum punishment?**

A) Identity theft — up to 3 years imprisonment
B) Violation of privacy — up to 3 years + ₹2 lakh fine
C) Cyber terrorism — up to life imprisonment ✅
D) Computer related offenses — up to 5 years imprisonment

> **Explanation:** **Section 66F = Cyber Terrorism** — acts using
> computer resources threatening national security, sovereignty, or
> causing damage to critical infrastructure — punishable by
> **up to life imprisonment**. This is the most severe punishment
> in the ITAA 2008 cybercrime provisions. Section 66C covers
> identity theft (3 years). Section 66E covers privacy violation.

---

**Q6. Section 66C of ITAA 2008 specifically criminalizes:**

A) Sending offensive messages through communication services
B) Capturing and publishing images of private areas
C) Fraudulent use of electronic signatures, passwords, or unique
   identification features — identity theft ✅
D) Publishing sexually explicit content online

> **Explanation:** **Section 66C = Identity Theft** — fraudulently
> or dishonestly making use of any other person's electronic
> signature, password, or any other unique identification feature.
> Punishment: up to 3 years + fine up to ₹1 lakh. Section 66A
> covered offensive messages (struck down). Section 66E covers
> privacy violation (capturing private images). Section 67A covers
> sexually explicit content.

---

**Q7. Section 66A of ITAA 2008 was struck down by the Supreme
Court of India in which landmark case and in which year?**

A) Maneka Gandhi vs Union of India — 2010
B) Shreya Singhal vs Union of India — 2015 ✅
C) KS Puttaswamy vs Union of India — 2017
D) PUCL vs Union of India — 2013

> **Explanation:** **Shreya Singhal vs Union of India (2015)** —
> the Supreme Court struck down Section 66A as unconstitutional
> because its terms ("grossly offensive," "menacing") were
> **vague** and had a chilling effect on free speech, violating
> Article 19(1)(a) of the Constitution. KS Puttaswamy vs Union
> of India (2017) established privacy as a fundamental right —
> a different but related landmark judgment.

**Q8. Section 70B of ITAA 2008 formally established which
organization as India's national nodal agency for cybersecurity?**

A) MeitY — Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology
B) NIC — National Informatics Centre
C) NASSCOM — National Association of Software Companies
D) CERT-In — Indian Computer Emergency Response Team ✅

> **Explanation:** **Section 70B formally established CERT-In
> (Indian Computer Emergency Response Team)** as India's national
> nodal agency for cybersecurity incident response. CERT-In operates
> under MeitY, runs a 24×7 helpdesk, issues cybersecurity advisories,
> and coordinates incident response nationally. MeitY is the
> administering ministry — not the nodal cybersecurity agency
> established by Section 70B.

---

**Q9. Section 79 of ITAA 2008 provides which protection to
internet intermediaries?**

A) Criminal immunity for all cybercrimes committed through their
   platforms
B) Safe harbor — exemption from liability if they exercise due
   diligence and comply with government directions ✅
C) Right to block any user without judicial oversight
D) Permission to collect and sell user data without consent

> **Explanation:** **Section 79 = Intermediary Safe Harbor** —
> internet intermediaries (social media, hosting providers, search
> engines) are NOT liable for third-party content if they:
> exercise **due diligence**, comply with **government notices**,
> and follow **IT (Intermediary Guidelines) Rules**. This is India's
> equivalent of Section 230 (US) or EU's e-Commerce Directive safe
> harbor provisions.

**Q10. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 (DPDPA)
received Presidential assent on which date?**

A) 5 February 2023
B) 27 October 2023
C) 1 January 2023
D) 11 August 2023 ✅

> **Explanation:** **DPDPA 2023 received Presidential assent on
> 11 August 2023** and was published in the Official Gazette on
> the same date. This date is directly tested in MCQs. The act
> is administered by MeitY and enforced through the Data Protection
> Board of India (DPBI). Note: while assented in August 2023, not
> all provisions are yet in force pending rules notification.

---

**Q11. DPDPA 2023 applies to which category of personal data —
making it narrower than GDPR?**

A) All personal data regardless of format — paper, oral, and digital
B) Only health-related personal data in digital form
C) Digital personal data only — paper and oral records are excluded ✅
D) Only personal data stored in cloud environments

> **Explanation:** **DPDPA 2023 applies only to DIGITAL personal
> data** — a significant limitation compared to GDPR which covers
> ALL forms of personal data processing including paper records.
> A physical paper medical record is NOT covered by DPDPA but
> the same data in an electronic health record IS covered.
> This narrower scope is one of DPDPA's key differences from GDPR.

---

**Q12. Under DPDPA 2023, the term "Data Principal" corresponds
to which GDPR term?**

A) Data Controller
B) Data Processor
C) Data Protection Officer
D) Data Subject ✅

> **Explanation:** **Data Principal (DPDPA) = Data Subject (GDPR)**
> — the individual whose personal data is being processed. The
> full DPDPA to GDPR terminology mapping is:
> Data Principal = Data Subject
> Data Fiduciary = Data Controller
> Data Processor = Data Processor (same)
> DPBI = DPA (national supervisory authority)
> Knowing these mappings is directly and frequently tested.

---

**Q13. Under DPDPA 2023, the entity that determines the purpose
and means of processing personal data is called:**

A) Data Principal
B) Data Processor
C) Consent Manager
D) Data Fiduciary ✅

> **Explanation:** **Data Fiduciary (DPDPA) = Data Controller
> (GDPR)** — the entity that determines the **purpose AND means**
> of processing personal data. The Data Fiduciary bears primary
> compliance responsibility under DPDPA. The Data Processor
> processes data on behalf of the Data Fiduciary. The Consent
> Manager is a new DPDPA-specific concept with no GDPR equivalent.

---

**Q14. How many rights does DPDPA 2023 grant to Data Principals
compared to GDPR's 8 rights?**

A) 8 rights — identical to GDPR
B) 6 rights — slightly fewer than GDPR
C) 4 rights — significantly fewer than GDPR ✅
D) 10 rights — more than GDPR

> **Explanation:** **DPDPA 2023 grants only 4 rights** to Data
> Principals — significantly fewer than GDPR's 8 rights. The 4
> DPDPA rights are: Right to Access Information, Right to
> Correction and Erasure, Right to Grievance Redressal, and Right
> to Nominate. Notably absent from DPDPA: Right to Data Portability,
> Right to Object, Right to Restrict Processing, and Automated
> Decision-Making rights.

---

**Q15. The "Right to Nominate" under DPDPA 2023 allows a Data
Principal to:**

A) Nominate a preferred Data Fiduciary for data processing
B) Nominate another person to exercise their data rights in case
   of death or incapacity ✅
C) Nominate a Consent Manager to manage all their consents
D) Nominate a representative to appear before the DPBI

> **Explanation:** The **Right to Nominate** under DPDPA 2023
> allows a Data Principal to designate another individual to
> exercise their data rights on their behalf in the event of
> **death or incapacity**. This is a unique right — no direct
> equivalent in GDPR. It addresses the practical issue of what
> happens to personal data when the data principal can no longer
> act for themselves.

---

**Q16. A "Consent Manager" under DPDPA 2023 is best described as:**

A) A government official who manages consent records
B) A Data Fiduciary's internal officer overseeing consent processes
C) A registered entity providing a single point of contact for
   Data Principals to give, manage, review, and withdraw consent ✅
D) An auditor appointed to verify consent compliance

> **Explanation:** The **Consent Manager** is a DPDPA-unique concept —
> a **registered entity** (registered with and regulated by DPBI)
> that provides a single interoperable platform through which Data
> Principals can manage their consent across multiple Data
> Fiduciaries. There is **no equivalent concept in GDPR** — making
> it one of the most distinctive and MCQ-worthy aspects of DPDPA 2023.

---

**Q17. A Data Fiduciary designated as a Significant Data Fiduciary
(SDF) under DPDPA 2023 must fulfill which additional obligations
not required of ordinary Data Fiduciaries?**

A) Obtain consent for all data processing including deemed consent
B) Appoint a DPO based in India, conduct periodic DPIA, and have
   an independent data auditor ✅
C) Store all data exclusively on Indian servers (data localization)
D) Delete all personal data within 30 days of collection

> **Explanation:** **Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDFs)** have
> additional obligations: **appoint a DPO based in India**,
> conduct **periodic Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs)**,
> and engage an **independent data auditor** to evaluate compliance.
> These mirror the heightened obligations for high-risk processors
> under GDPR. The Central Government designates SDFs based on
> volume/sensitivity of data, risk to rights, and national security
> implications.

**Q18. The Data Protection Board of India (DPBI) under DPDPA 2023
functions primarily as:**

A) A government ministry overseeing all digital policies
B) A statutory adjudicatory body that adjudicates complaints,
   investigates violations, and imposes penalties ✅
C) A certification body for Data Fiduciary compliance
D) An advisory committee recommending data protection policies

> **Explanation:** **DPBI = Data Protection Board of India** —
> a statutory body that **adjudicates** complaints from Data
> Principals, **investigates** DPDPA violations, and **imposes
> financial penalties**. It functions as a **digital office** —
> proceedings conducted through digital means. DPBI decisions
> are appealable to the High Court. Unlike GDPR's DPAs which
> have broader supervisory powers, DPBI is primarily an
> adjudicatory body.

**Q19. The maximum financial penalty under DPDPA 2023 is ₹250
crore — applicable to which type of violation?**

A) Non-compliance with DPBI orders
B) Non-compliance with SDF-specific obligations
C) Failure to notify a data breach to DPBI
D) Failure to implement security safeguards resulting in a
   data breach ✅

> **Explanation:** The **maximum DPDPA penalty of ₹250 crore**
> applies to **failure to implement security safeguards that
> results in a data breach** — the most serious data protection
> failure. Other penalty amounts: ₹200 crore for children's data
> violations and failure to notify breaches, ₹150 crore for
> SDF obligation failures, ₹50 crore for DPBI order non-compliance.

**Q20. How does DPDPA 2023's approach to cross-border data
transfer fundamentally differ from GDPR's approach?**

A) DPDPA prohibits all cross-border transfers — GDPR allows them
B) DPDPA uses an allowlist (permitted countries) — GDPR uses a
   blocklist (restricted countries)
C) DPDPA uses a blocklist (restricted countries) — GDPR uses an
   allowlist (adequacy decisions) ✅
D) Both DPDPA and GDPR use identical adequacy decision frameworks

> **Explanation:** **DPDPA = blocklist approach** — data can be
> transferred to ANY country EXCEPT those on a government-issued
> restricted list. **GDPR = allowlist approach** — transfers
> only permitted to countries with an adequacy decision, or using
> SCCs/BCRs. DPDPA's approach is more **business-friendly** and
> less restrictive than GDPR's conservative default-restrict model.

**Q21. CERT-In issued mandatory cybersecurity incident reporting
directions in April 2022 requiring organizations to report
incidents within what timeframe?**

A) 24 hours of discovery
B) 72 hours of discovery
C) 6 hours of noticing the incident ✅
D) 48 hours of discovery

> **Explanation:** **CERT-In's 2022 mandatory directions require
> reporting within 6 hours** of noticing a cybersecurity incident —
> the strictest mandatory reporting timeline among major frameworks.
> Compare: GDPR = 72 hours (to DPA), HIPAA = 60 days (to HHS/
> individuals). The CERT-In 6-hour rule is controversial but legally
> mandatory for organizations operating in India. This three-way
> comparison (6 hrs / 72 hrs / 60 days) is directly tested.

**Q22. The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) was enacted in
which year and by which country, and covers which type of bribery?**

A) 2010 — UK — all bribery including commercial
B) 1977 — USA — bribing foreign government officials only ✅
C) 1977 — USA — all bribery including commercial transactions
D) 2010 — USA — bribing domestic and foreign government officials

> **Explanation:** **FCPA = Foreign Corrupt Practices Act** —
> enacted by the **USA in 1977** — covers **bribing foreign
> government officials only**. It does NOT cover commercial
> bribery between private parties. The UKBA (2010) covers ALL
> bribery including commercial. Knowing FCPA = USA + 1977 +
> government officials only is directly tested alongside the
> UKBA comparison.

**Q23. The UK Bribery Act (UKBA) 2010 is considered stricter than
the FCPA primarily because:**

A) UKBA has higher financial penalties than FCPA
B) UKBA covers all bribery including commercial bribery and
   prohibits facilitation payments that FCPA allows ✅
C) UKBA applies to more countries than FCPA
D) UKBA requires mandatory ISO 37001 certification

> **Explanation:** **UKBA is stricter than FCPA in two key ways:**
> 1. **Broader scope** — UKBA covers ALL bribery (government +
> commercial) while FCPA covers only foreign government official
> bribery. 2. **Facilitation payments prohibited** — UKBA
> prohibits small facilitation payments (common in some countries)
> while FCPA has a narrow exception for them. The Section 7
> UKBA corporate offense for failure to prevent bribery also
> makes UKBA more impactful for organizations.

---

**Q24. ISO 37001 is the international standard for which
management system relevant to the anti-corruption case study?**

A) Information security management systems
B) Anti-bribery management systems ✅
C) Quality management systems
D) Environmental management systems

> **Explanation:** **ISO 37001 = Anti-Bribery Management Systems**
> standard — it provides a framework for organizations to prevent,
> detect, and respond to bribery. Under UKBA, having "adequate
> procedures" (equivalent to ISO 37001) is a defense against
> corporate liability. ISO 27001 = information security management.
> ISO 9001 = quality management. ISO 14001 = environmental
> management.

**Q25. In the Global Bank internal audit case study, which
framework was primarily used for assessing Internal Controls
over Financial Reporting (ICFR) for SOX compliance?**

A) COBIT
B) ISO 27001
C) COSO ✅
D) NIST CSF

> **Explanation:** **COSO (Committee of Sponsoring Organizations)**
> is the primary framework for SOX 404 ICFR assessment — as
> established in Session 06 (SOX & SOC). In the Global Bank
> case study, COSO was used for financial internal controls,
> COBIT for IT governance and IT audit methodology, ISO 27001
> for information security, PCI DSS for payment card data, and
> NIST CSF for cybersecurity risk management. Each framework
> served a specific purpose.

**Q26. In anti-corruption compliance audits, "facilitation
payments" are:**

A) Legitimate business expenses for government contract bids
B) Small unofficial payments to expedite routine government
   actions — allowed under FCPA but prohibited under UKBA ✅
C) Payments made to whistleblowers for reporting corruption
D) Mandatory audit fees paid to government regulators

> **Explanation:** **Facilitation payments** (also called "grease
> payments") are small unofficial payments to government officials
> to speed up routine actions (permits, licenses, customs clearance).
> **FCPA allows them** under a narrow exception. **UKBA
> prohibits them entirely** — making UKBA stricter. This
> distinction is a common MCQ trap and a key practical difference
> between the two anti-corruption frameworks.

**Q27. Which of the following is a key "red flag" in an
anti-corruption compliance audit that should trigger deeper
investigation?**

A) Payments to well-known established vendors with long
   business history
B) Unusually high commissions paid to agents operating in
   government contract territories ✅
C) Standard employee salary payments approved by HR
D) Routine utility payments for office premises

> **Explanation:** **Unusually high commissions to agents** is
> a classic anti-corruption red flag — agents may be using
> inflated commissions to fund bribes to government officials
> on behalf of the company. Other red flags include payments
> to shell companies, cash payments, vague invoices, payments
> to government officials' relatives, and no-bid contract wins.
> All standard vendor, employee, and utility payments are
> normal business activities.

**Q28. What was the name of the committee whose 2018 report
provided the foundational recommendations for India's data
protection law — leading eventually to DPDPA 2023?**

A) IT Act Review Committee
B) Cyber Law Review Committee
C) Justice BN Srikrishna Committee ✅
D) NASSCOM Data Protection Committee

> **Explanation:** The **Justice BN Srikrishna Committee** (formally
> the Expert Committee on Data Protection) produced a comprehensive
> 2018 report recommending India's first data protection law —
> the Personal Data Protection Bill (PDPB). This bill went through
> multiple revisions (2019 PDPB, 2022 draft DPDPA) before the
> DPDPA 2023 was finally enacted. The Srikrishna Committee report
> was heavily influenced by GDPR principles.

**Q29. DPDPA 2023 requires parental consent for processing data
of children below which age — and how does this differ from
GDPR?**

A) DPDPA = under 16 / GDPR = under 18
B) DPDPA = under 13 / GDPR = under 16
C) DPDPA = under 18 / GDPR = under 16 ✅
D) Both DPDPA and GDPR use under 18 as the threshold

> **Explanation:** **DPDPA sets the children's age threshold at
> under 18** — requiring verifiable parental consent for processing
> children's data. **GDPR sets the threshold at under 16** (with
> member states able to lower it to 13). DPDPA's higher threshold
> (18 vs 16) is one of its more conservative provisions — meaning
> more individuals require parental consent in India than under GDPR.

**Q30. Which combination of ITAA 2008 and DPDPA 2023 facts is
entirely correct?**

A) ITAA 2008 replaced IT Act 2000 / Section 66F = identity theft /
   DPDPA enacted 2022 / Data Principal = Data Controller /
   DPDPA has 8 rights / max penalty ₹100 crore
B) ITAA 2008 amended IT Act 2000 / Section 66F = cyber terrorism /
   DPDPA enacted 2023 / Data Principal = Data Subject /
   DPDPA has 4 rights / max penalty ₹250 crore ✅
C) ITAA 2008 amended IT Act 2000 / Section 66C = cyber terrorism /
   DPDPA enacted 2023 / Data Fiduciary = Data Subject /
   DPDPA has 4 rights / max penalty ₹250 crore
D) ITAA 2008 replaced IT Act 2000 / Section 70B = data protection /
   DPDPA enacted 2023 / Data Principal = Data Subject /
   DPDPA has 6 rights / max penalty ₹200 crore

> **Explanation:** The fully correct combination is:
> **ITAA 2008 amended** (not replaced) IT Act 2000 ✅
> **Section 66F = cyber terrorism** ✅ (66C = identity theft)
> **DPDPA enacted 2023** ✅ (Presidential assent 11 August 2023)
> **Data Principal = Data Subject** ✅ (Data Fiduciary = Controller)
> **DPDPA has 4 rights** ✅ (not 8 — that's GDPR)
> **Max penalty ₹250 crore** ✅ (for security safeguard failure)
> Option C fails because Data Fiduciary = Data Controller (not
> Data Subject). Option D fails because 70B = CERT-In (not data
> protection) and DPDPA has 4 rights (not 6).
