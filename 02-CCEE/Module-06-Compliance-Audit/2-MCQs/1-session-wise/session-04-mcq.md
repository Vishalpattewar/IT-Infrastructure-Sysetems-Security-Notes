## MCQ Set

---

**Q1. GDPR stands for and came into enforcement on which date?**

A) General Data Privacy Regulation — 25 May 2016
B) Global Data Protection Regulation — 27 April 2016
C) General Data Protection Regulation — 25 May 2018 ✅
D) General Data Protection Regulation — 25 May 2016

> **Explanation:** **GDPR = General Data Protection Regulation** and it
> came into **enforcement on 25 May 2018** — after a 2-year transition
> period following its adoption on 27 April 2016. The regulation number
> is EU 2016/679. A common trap is confusing the adoption date (2016)
> with the enforcement date (2018).

---

**Q2. GDPR is classified as a Regulation rather than a Directive. The
key significance of this is:**

A) It applies only to public sector organizations
B) It applies directly and uniformly across all EU member states without
   national legislation ✅
C) It requires each EU country to pass its own national law to implement it
D) It applies only to organizations headquartered in the EU

> **Explanation:** A **Regulation** is directly applicable across all EU
> member states automatically — no national legislation needed. A
> **Directive** (like the old 95/46/EC) requires each country to pass its
> own implementing law. This is a critical legal distinction and a very
> common MCQ trap.

---

**Q3. A technology company based in India operates a website that offers
services to users in France and Germany. Does GDPR apply to this
company?**

A) No — GDPR only applies to companies physically located in the EU
B) No — GDPR only applies to EU-based companies processing EU data
C) Yes — GDPR applies because the company targets EU residents ✅
D) Yes — but only to its EU-based employees' data

> **Explanation:** GDPR has **extraterritorial reach** via the **Targeting
> Criterion** — any organization outside the EU that offers goods or
> services to EU residents must comply, regardless of where the company
> is located. Geographic location of the company does NOT exempt it.

---

**Q4. Under GDPR, which of the following is considered personal data?**

A) Anonymised statistical data published in a research report
B) Aggregated sales figures with no individual identifiers
C) A user's IP address collected by a website ✅
D) General demographic trends about a city's population

> **Explanation:** Under GDPR, an **IP address is personal data** because
> it can be used to identify a natural person. Anonymised data and
> aggregated data with no individual identifiers are NOT personal data
> under GDPR. This is broader than many people expect and a common trap.

---

**Q5. Which of the following is classified as a Special Category of
data under GDPR requiring extra protection?**

A) Financial transaction records
B) Email address and phone number
C) Home address and postcode
D) Genetic data ✅

> **Explanation:** **Genetic data** is one of the 8 Special Categories
> under GDPR Article 9. Financial records, email addresses, phone numbers,
> and addresses are personal data but NOT special categories. Special
> categories are sensitive types that face a general prohibition on
> processing unless specific exceptions apply.

---

**Q6. How many Special Categories of sensitive personal data does GDPR
define under Article 9?**

A) 6
B) 7
C) 8 ✅
D) 10

> **Explanation:** GDPR Article 9 defines exactly **8 Special Categories**:
> racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious/philosophical beliefs,
> trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data (for unique ID),
> health data, and sex life/sexual orientation. This exact number is
> frequently tested in MCQs.

---

**Q7. Which of the following is NOT one of the 8 Special Categories of
data under GDPR Article 9?**

A) Trade union membership
B) Biometric data used for unique identification
C) Criminal conviction records ✅
D) Religious or philosophical beliefs

> **Explanation:** **Criminal conviction and offence data** is covered
> separately under **Article 10** — it is NOT one of the 8 Special
> Categories under Article 9. It receives similar strong protection but
> is treated as a separate category. Trade union membership, biometric
> data (for ID), and religious beliefs ARE all Special Categories.

---

**Q8. GDPR Article 5 defines how many core principles for processing
personal data?**

A) 5
B) 6
C) 7 ✅
D) 8

> **Explanation:** GDPR Article 5 defines exactly **7 core principles**:
> Lawfulness/Fairness/Transparency, Purpose Limitation, Data Minimisation,
> Accuracy, Storage Limitation, Integrity and Confidentiality, and
> Accountability. The number 7 is directly tested in MCQs.

---

**Q9. The GDPR principle that states organizations must only collect data
that is adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary is:**

A) Purpose Limitation
B) Accuracy
C) Storage Limitation
D) Data Minimisation ✅

> **Explanation:** **Data Minimisation** = collect only what you need —
> adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary. Purpose Limitation
> = use data only for its stated purpose. Storage Limitation = don't keep
> data longer than needed. These three are often confused in MCQs.

---

**Q10. Which GDPR principle requires that organizations must be able to
demonstrate their compliance with all other principles?**

A) Integrity and Confidentiality
B) Lawfulness, Fairness and Transparency
C) Purpose Limitation
D) Accountability ✅

> **Explanation:** **Accountability** (Principle 7) is the most significant
> new principle GDPR added compared to the old Directive. It requires
> organizations to not just comply — but to **prove and document** that
> they comply. This drives all record-keeping and documentation
> requirements in GDPR.

---

**Q11. An organization collects customer email addresses for order
confirmations but then uses the same emails for marketing campaigns
without informing customers. This violates which GDPR principle?**

A) Data Minimisation
B) Accuracy
C) Purpose Limitation ✅
D) Storage Limitation

> **Explanation:** **Purpose Limitation** = data must be used only for
> the specific purpose it was collected for. Using emails collected for
> order confirmations for marketing is a different purpose — a clear
> Purpose Limitation violation. Data Minimisation would be about
> collecting too much data, not using it for the wrong purpose.

---

**Q12. How many rights does GDPR give to data subjects?**

A) 6
B) 7
C) 8 ✅
D) 9

> **Explanation:** GDPR gives data subjects exactly **8 rights**:
> Right to be Informed, Right of Access, Right to Rectification, Right
> to Erasure, Right to Restrict Processing, Right to Data Portability,
> Right to Object, and Rights regarding Automated Decision Making.

---

**Q13. The "Right to be Forgotten" under GDPR is formally known as:**

A) Right to Restrict Processing
B) Right to Object
C) Right to Data Portability
D) Right to Erasure ✅

> **Explanation:** The **Right to Erasure = Right to be Forgotten** —
> Article 17. It allows individuals to request deletion of their
> personal data. However, it is NOT absolute — exceptions exist for
> legal obligations, public interest, and freedom of expression. The
> nickname "Right to be Forgotten" appears frequently in MCQs.

---

**Q14. Under GDPR, the Right to Data Portability allows individuals to:**

A) Move their data to any country in the world freely
B) Request all organizations delete their data simultaneously
C) Receive their data in a structured, machine-readable format and
   transfer it to another controller ✅
D) Prevent any organization from storing their data

> **Explanation:** **Right to Data Portability (Article 20)** = receive
> your personal data in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable
> format AND have the right to transmit it to another controller. It
> applies only when processing is based on consent or contract AND
> carried out by automated means. It was a new right introduced by GDPR.

---

**Q15. An individual receives targeted political advertising via email
without having consented to it. They can invoke which GDPR right to
stop it?**

A) Right to Erasure
B) Right to Rectification
C) Right to Object ✅
D) Right to Restrict Processing

> **Explanation:** The **Right to Object (Article 21)** allows individuals
> to object to processing — especially for **direct marketing purposes**.
> When an individual objects to direct marketing, processing MUST stop
> with no exceptions. Right to Restrict would pause processing but not
> stop it. Right to Erasure would delete data entirely.

---

**Q16. How many lawful bases for processing personal data does GDPR
Article 6 define?**

A) 4
B) 5
C) 6 ✅
D) 7

> **Explanation:** GDPR Article 6 defines exactly **6 lawful bases**:
> Consent, Contract, Legal Obligation, Vital Interests, Public Task,
> and Legitimate Interests. Organizations must identify and document
> at least one lawful basis before processing personal data.

---

**Q17. A hospital processes patient health data without obtaining
explicit consent because it is required by law for public health
reporting. Which lawful basis applies?**

A) Consent
B) Contract
C) Vital Interests
D) Legal Obligation ✅

> **Explanation:** **Legal Obligation** = processing necessary to comply
> with a legal requirement. Mandatory public health reporting imposed
> by law does not require patient consent — the law itself provides the
> lawful basis. Vital Interests applies to life-or-death emergencies
> when consent cannot be obtained.

---

**Q18. A common misconception about GDPR is that organizations always
need consent to process personal data. This is:**

A) Correct — consent is always required under GDPR
B) Incorrect — there are 6 lawful bases and consent is just one of them ✅
C) Correct — consent is required except for government organizations
D) Incorrect — consent is never sufficient on its own

> **Explanation:** **Consent is just ONE of 6 lawful bases** — this is
> one of the most important GDPR misconceptions to correct. Organizations
> often unnecessarily rely on consent when another basis (contract, legal
> obligation, legitimate interests) would be more appropriate. Also,
> consent must be freely given — it cannot be a condition of service.

---

**Q19. The entity that determines the PURPOSE and MEANS of processing
personal data under GDPR is called the:**

A) Data Processor
B) Data Subject
C) Data Protection Officer
D) Data Controller ✅

> **Explanation:** The **Data Controller** decides WHY (purpose) and HOW
> (means) personal data is processed and bears primary GDPR compliance
> responsibility. The Data Processor processes data ON BEHALF of the
> controller as instructed. The DPO monitors compliance but does not
> control processing.

---

**Q20. Which of the following correctly distinguishes a Data Controller
from a Data Processor?**

A) Controller processes data; Processor decides why to process it
B) Controller decides why and how; Processor acts on controller's
   instructions ✅
C) Controller is always a government body; Processor is always private
D) Controller and Processor are different names for the same role

> **Explanation:** **Controller = decides WHY and HOW.** **Processor =
> acts as instructed by the controller.** A cloud storage provider
> storing customer data on behalf of a business is a Processor. The
> business that decides to use cloud storage and what data to store
> is the Controller.

---

**Q21. For which of the following organizations is appointing a Data
Protection Officer (DPO) mandatory under GDPR?**

A) Any company with more than 50 employees
B) Any company with an annual turnover exceeding €1 million
C) Any public authority or body ✅
D) Any company operating a website with EU visitors

> **Explanation:** DPO appointment is mandatory for: **public authorities
> or bodies**, organizations carrying out large-scale systematic monitoring
> of individuals, and organizations carrying out large-scale processing of
> special category data. Company size, turnover, and simply having a
> website are NOT the triggers.

---

**Q22. The DPO under GDPR must report to:**

A) The Data Protection Authority (DPA)
B) The IT Security Manager
C) The Legal Counsel
D) The highest level of management ✅

> **Explanation:** The **DPO must report directly to the highest level of
> management** — the Board or CEO level. This ensures their independence.
> They cannot receive instructions on how to perform their DPO tasks from
> line managers or department heads. Reporting to IT or Legal would
> compromise their independence.

---

**Q23. Under GDPR, when a personal data breach occurs, the Data
Protection Authority (DPA) must be notified within:**

A) 24 hours
B) 48 hours
C) 72 hours ✅
D) 7 days

> **Explanation:** **72 hours** — this is the single most tested number
> in GDPR MCQs. Article 33 requires notification to the DPA within
> **72 hours of becoming aware** of the breach (where it is likely to
> result in risk to individuals' rights and freedoms). Not 24, not 48,
> not 7 days — **72 hours**.

---

**Q24. Under GDPR, individual data subjects must be notified about a
personal data breach:**

A) Within 72 hours of the breach occurring
B) Within 48 hours of notifying the DPA
C) Only when the breach involves special category data
D) Without undue delay — but only when the breach is likely to result
   in HIGH risk to them ✅

> **Explanation:** Individual notification has a **two-part condition**:
> it must happen **without undue delay** AND only when the breach is
> **likely to result in HIGH risk** to the rights and freedoms of the
> individuals concerned. If risk is low or data was encrypted, individual
> notification may not be required. The 72-hour rule applies to DPA
> notification, not individual notification.

---

**Q25. An organization suffers a data breach but the stolen data was
fully encrypted and the encryption key was not compromised. Under GDPR,
the organization:**

A) Must still notify the DPA within 72 hours regardless
B) Must notify all affected individuals immediately
C) May not need to notify individuals as risk is unlikely ✅
D) Must notify both DPA and individuals within 24 hours

> **Explanation:** GDPR notification requirements are **risk-based**. If
> the data was encrypted and the key is safe, **re-identification is
> impossible** — meaning risk to individuals is unlikely. In this case,
> individual notification may not be required. The organization should
> still assess and document the breach internally. DPA notification may
> also be unnecessary if risk to individuals is unlikely.

---

**Q26. The maximum fine under GDPR for the most serious violations
(upper tier) is:**

A) €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover
B) €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover ✅
C) €50 million or 4% of global annual turnover
D) €20 million or 2% of global annual turnover

> **Explanation:** The **upper tier fine = €20 million OR 4% of global
> annual turnover** — whichever is HIGHER. This applies to the most
> serious violations including breach of core principles, data subject
> rights, and international transfer rules. The lower tier is €10M/2%
> for less serious procedural violations.

---

**Q27. GDPR Article 25 requires organizations to implement Data
Protection by Design and by Default. "By Default" specifically means:**

A) Applying security patches by default on all systems
B) Privacy-protective settings must be the default without user action
   required ✅
C) All data must be encrypted by default
D) Data must be deleted by default after 30 days

> **Explanation:** **Privacy by Default** = the most privacy-protective
> settings must be the default option — users should not need to opt out
> of privacy protections; privacy protection should be the starting
> position. **Privacy by Design** = building privacy into systems from
> the architecture stage. These are two distinct but related concepts.

---

**Q28. A DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment) is mandatory before
starting processing that is:**

A) Carried out by a small business with fewer than 50 employees
B) Related to any financial transaction data
C) Likely to result in HIGH risk to individuals' rights and freedoms ✅
D) Required for any processing of personal data

> **Explanation:** A **DPIA is mandatory when processing is LIKELY to
> result in HIGH risk** to individuals' rights and freedoms — Article 35.
> Specific triggers include large-scale processing of special categories,
> systematic monitoring of public areas, large-scale profiling, and use
> of new technologies. It is NOT required for all processing — only
> high-risk activities.

---

**Q29. The primary mechanism currently used for transferring personal
data from the EU to the United States after the invalidation of the
Privacy Shield is:**

A) Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)
B) Adequacy Decision
C) Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) ✅
D) Explicit Consent

> **Explanation:** The **US-EU Privacy Shield was invalidated by the
> Schrems II ruling in 2020**. **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)**
> are now the primary mechanism for EU-US data transfers. BCRs apply
> to intra-group multinational transfers. The US does not currently
> have a full EU adequacy decision (the EU-US Data Privacy Framework
> is now in place but SCCs remain widely used).

---

**Q30. Which combination of GDPR elements is correctly matched?**

A) Article 5 → Lawful Bases / Article 6 → Core Principles /
   Article 33 → DPO Designation
B) Article 5 → Core Principles / Article 6 → Lawful Bases /
   Article 33 → Breach Notification to DPA ✅
C) Article 5 → Data Subject Rights / Article 9 → Core Principles /
   Article 35 → Fines
D) Article 6 → Special Categories / Article 9 → Lawful Bases /
   Article 83 → Breach Notification

> **Explanation:** The correct mapping is:
> **Article 5 → 7 Core Principles**
> **Article 6 → 6 Lawful Bases for Processing**
> **Article 33 → Breach Notification to DPA (72 hours)**
> Article 9 = Special Categories. Article 35 = DPIA. Article 37 = DPO.
> Article 83 = Fines. Knowing article numbers is directly tested.
