## Answers & Explanations

---

**Q1. Correct Answer: D) Accountability** ✅

**Explanation:**
The CIA Triad consists of exactly three pillars: **Confidentiality**, **Integrity**, and **Availability**. These are the three foundational properties of information security.

- **A) Confidentiality** — ❌ This IS part of the CIA Triad
- **B) Integrity** — ❌ This IS part of the CIA Triad
- **C) Availability** — ❌ This IS part of the CIA Triad
- **D) Accountability** — ✅ This is part of the EXTENDED model beyond the CIA Triad (along with Authentication, Non-Repudiation, Privacy) — but NOT one of the three core pillars

> The extended model adds Accountability, Authentication, Non-Repudiation, and Privacy — but the base triad is always CIA only.

---

**Q2. Correct Answer: B) Tampering** ✅

**Explanation:**
STRIDE is a threat modeling framework where each letter represents a specific threat category:
**S**poofing · **T**ampering · **R**epudiation · **I**nformation Disclosure · **D**enial of Service · **E**levation of Privilege

- **A) Threat** — ❌ "T" does not stand for Threat in STRIDE — the entire model is about threats
- **B) Tampering** — ✅ Correct — modifying data without authorization
- **C) Tracking** — ❌ Not a STRIDE category at all
- **D) Tunneling** — ❌ Not a STRIDE category — tunneling is a network technique

---

**Q3. Correct Answer: C) Never trust, always verify — regardless of network location** ✅

**Explanation:**
Zero Trust Architecture is defined by its core principle: **"Never Trust, Always Verify."** Every access request must be authenticated, authorized, and continuously validated — even if it comes from inside the corporate network.

- **A)** — ❌ This is the OLD perimeter security model — exactly what ZTA replaces
- **B)** — ❌ Trusting once past the firewall is the failed castle-and-moat model
- **C)** — ✅ Correct definition of Zero Trust
- **D)** — ❌ "Trust but verify" is a softer model — ZTA says NEVER trust by default; always verify

---

**Q4. Correct Answer: C) Detective** ✅

**Explanation:**
Security controls are classified by their function:
- **Preventive** — Stop the attack before it happens (firewalls, encryption)
- **Detective** — Identify and DETECT attacks that are happening or have happened (audit logs, IDS, SIEM)
- **Corrective** — Fix the damage after detection (patch management, IR)
- **Deterrent** — Discourage attacks (warning banners, cameras)

Audit logs and SIEM alerts **detect and record** — they do not stop the attack. Therefore they are **Detective** controls.

- **A)** — ❌ Preventive controls stop attacks BEFORE they happen
- **B)** — ❌ Corrective controls fix things AFTER an attack
- **C)** — ✅ Detective controls identify and alert on attacks
- **D)** — ❌ Deterrent controls discourage but don't detect

---

**Q5. Correct Answer: D) Risk Transfer** ✅

**Explanation:**
The four risk treatment options are: Accept · Mitigate · Transfer · Avoid

**Risk Transfer** means shifting the financial burden of a risk to a third party — the most common example is purchasing cyber insurance or outsourcing a risky function.

- **A) Avoidance** — ❌ Stopping the risky activity entirely (e.g., not storing credit card data at all)
- **B) Acceptance** — ❌ Living with the risk — no action taken
- **C) Mitigation** — ❌ Reducing the likelihood or impact through controls
- **D) Transfer** — ✅ Moving the financial risk to an insurer or third party

---

**Q6. Correct Answer: C) Whaling** ✅

**Explanation:**
Social engineering targeting types:
- **Phishing** — Broad, mass-sent deceptive emails to any recipients
- **Spear Phishing** — Targeted at a specific individual or small group
- **Whaling** — Targeted specifically at C-suite executives (CEO, CFO, CISO)

The key differentiator is the **target being a C-suite executive** — that's the definition of Whaling.

- **A) Phishing** — ❌ Mass targeting, not executive-specific
- **B) Spear Phishing** — ❌ Targeted but not exclusively C-suite
- **C) Whaling** — ✅ Specifically targets high-value executives
- **D) Vishing** — ❌ Voice phishing — wrong delivery channel

---

**Q7. Correct Answer: D) Urgency** ✅

**Explanation:**
Social engineering exploits psychological triggers. The phrase *"5 minutes before permanent deletion"* creates immediate time pressure — this is the **Urgency** principle. Urgency bypasses rational thinking and forces hasty, unconsidered action.

- **A) Authority** — ❌ Authority uses power/rank ("I'm the IT Director")
- **B) Social Proof** — ❌ Social proof uses peer behavior ("everyone has already done this")
- **C) Reciprocity** — ❌ Reciprocity uses past favors ("I helped you last week")
- **D) Urgency** — ✅ Time pressure forcing immediate action

---

**Q8. Correct Answer: B) Preparation → Detection → Containment → Eradication → Recovery → Lessons Learned** ✅

**Explanation:**
The NIST 800-61 Incident Response Lifecycle has a specific order. **Preparation always comes first** — you must have policies, tools, and a team BEFORE an incident occurs. Detection follows, then Containment, Eradication, Recovery, and finally Lessons Learned.

- **A)** — ❌ Starts with Detection — you cannot detect before you prepare
- **B)** — ✅ Correct order: Prep → Detect → Contain → Eradicate → Recover → Learn
- **C)** — ❌ Puts Containment before Detection — illogical
- **D)** — ❌ Starts with Detection and puts Preparation third — incorrect

---

**Q9. Correct Answer: C) Layered security so that if one control fails, others continue to protect** ✅

**Explanation:**
Defense in Depth (DiD) is the layered security concept — Physical → Network → Host → Application → Data. The key idea is **no single point of failure** — even if an attacker breaches one layer, additional layers remain.

- **A)** — ❌ A single perimeter firewall is the OPPOSITE of DiD
- **B)** — ❌ Only detective controls is one-dimensional — not layered
- **C)** — ✅ Multiple layers where each layer is an additional barrier
- **D)** — ❌ Encryption is ONE layer of DiD — not the full concept

---

**Q10. Correct Answer: B) Collection** ✅

**Explanation:**
The MITRE ATT&CK kill chain phases in order include:
...Discovery → **Lateral Movement** → **Collection** → Command & Control → Exfiltration → Impact

After moving through the network (Lateral Movement), the attacker gathers data of interest (Collection) before exfiltrating it.

- **A) Persistence** — ❌ Persistence comes much earlier — after Initial Access
- **B) Collection** — ✅ Comes directly after Lateral Movement
- **C) Exfiltration** — ❌ Comes after Collection, not immediately after Lateral Movement
- **D) Initial Access** — ❌ This is near the beginning of the kill chain

---

**Q11. Correct Answer: C) WEP** ✅

**Explanation:**
**WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy)** was introduced in 1997 and uses the RC4 stream cipher with a 40-bit or 104-bit key. It has been cryptographically broken for many years — it can be cracked in minutes using tools like Aircrack-ng. It must NEVER be used.

- **A) WPA2-Personal** — ❌ Uses AES-CCMP — acceptable minimum as of 2026
- **B) WPA3-Personal** — ❌ Uses SAE (Dragonfly) — RECOMMENDED in 2026
- **C) WEP** — ✅ Uses RC4 — completely broken — never use
- **D) WPA-TKIP** — ❌ Also deprecated but uses TKIP over RC4 — still more secure than WEP

---

**Q12. Correct Answer: C) SAE (Simultaneous Authentication of Equals / Dragonfly)** ✅

**Explanation:**
WPA3-Personal replaced the vulnerable PSK (Pre-Shared Key) handshake with **SAE — Simultaneous Authentication of Equals**, also called the Dragonfly handshake. SAE prevents offline dictionary attacks even if the attacker captures the handshake.

- **A) TKIP** — ❌ Used in WPA (2003) — replaced by AES
- **B) AES-CCMP** — ❌ Used in WPA2 — the encryption algorithm, not the handshake mechanism
- **C) SAE** — ✅ WPA3's new handshake — replaces PSK
- **D) RADIUS/802.1X** — ❌ Used in WPA2/WPA3-Enterprise, not Personal mode

---

**Q13. Correct Answer: D) Evil Twin Attack** ✅

**Explanation:**
An **Evil Twin Attack** involves setting up a rogue access point with the same SSID (network name) as a legitimate network. Victims connect to it believing it's real, while the attacker intercepts all their traffic.

- **A) War Driving** — ❌ War driving is searching FOR Wi-Fi networks from a moving vehicle — not creating a fake one
- **B) Deauthentication Attack** — ❌ Forces clients off a network by flooding deauth frames — does not create a rogue AP
- **C) KRACK Attack** — ❌ Key Reinstallation Attack — targets WPA2's four-way handshake
- **D) Evil Twin Attack** — ✅ Rogue AP with same SSID as legitimate network

**Q14. Correct Answer: C) 12** ✅

**Explanation:**
PCI DSS v4.0 is organized around **6 goals** and **12 requirements**. This structure has been consistent across versions of PCI DSS. Each requirement maps to one of the six security goals (Build secure network, Protect cardholder data, etc.).

- **A) 6** — ❌ 6 is the number of GOALS — not requirements
- **B) 8** — ❌ Incorrect — no version of PCI DSS has 8 requirements
- **C) 12** — ✅ PCI DSS v4.0 has exactly 12 requirements
- **D) 20** — ❌ Incorrect — 20 controls is associated with other frameworks (CIS Controls)

---

**Q15. Correct Answer: B) SIM Swap Attack** ✅

**Explanation:**
A **SIM Swap Attack** (also called SIM Swapping or SIM Hijacking) involves convincing a mobile carrier to transfer the victim's phone number to a SIM card controlled by the attacker. This gives the attacker full control of SMS messages — including OTPs for SMS-based 2FA.

- **A) SIM Cloning** — ❌ Physically duplicating a SIM card — different technique, requires hardware
- **B) SIM Swap** — ✅ Social engineering the carrier to port the number
- **C) IMSI Catching** — ❌ Uses a fake cell tower (IMSI catcher/Stingray) — different attack entirely
- **D) SS7 Attack** — ❌ Exploits Signaling System No. 7 telecom protocols at the network level

---

**Q16. Correct Answer: C) Tokenization** ✅

**Explanation:**
**Tokenization** replaces sensitive data (like a credit card Primary Account Number/PAN) with a non-sensitive substitute called a **token**. The token is meaningless to an attacker even if intercepted. Apple Pay, Google Pay, and Samsung Pay all use tokenization.

- **A) Encryption** — ❌ Encryption transforms data but can be decrypted with the key — tokenization has no mathematical relationship between token and original value
- **B) Hashing** — ❌ Hashing is a one-way function used for integrity — not for payment data replacement
- **C) Tokenization** — ✅ Replaces PAN with a useless token
- **D) Obfuscation** — ❌ Makes data harder to read but does not provide strong protection

---

**Q17. Correct Answer: C) RBAC (Role-Based Access Control)** ✅

**Explanation:**
**RBAC** assigns permissions based on job roles within an organization (e.g., "Finance Manager," "HR Analyst," "Database Admin"). It is the most commonly deployed access control model in enterprise environments because it scales well and matches how organizations actually work.

- **A) DAC** — ❌ Owner-controlled permissions — more common in personal OS environments like Windows NTFS
- **B) MAC** — ❌ System-enforced classification labels — used in military/government (SELinux)
- **C) RBAC** — ✅ Role-based — most common in enterprise environments
- **D) ABAC** — ❌ Attribute-based — more fine-grained and context-aware — more complex than RBAC

---

**Q18. Correct Answer: C) MD5** ✅

**Explanation:**
**MD5 (Message Digest 5)** is cryptographically broken — collision attacks have been demonstrated, and its hashes can be cracked rapidly with rainbow tables and modern GPUs. It should NEVER be used for password storage.

- **A) SHA-256** — ❌ SHA-256 is currently secure and widely used
- **B) SHA-3** — ❌ SHA-3 is current and secure
- **C) MD5** — ✅ Broken — do not use for passwords or security purposes
- **D) bcrypt** — ❌ bcrypt is specifically designed for password hashing and remains secure

> SHA-1 is also deprecated (broken for collision resistance) — but MD5 is the most clearly broken of the listed options.

---

**Q19. Correct Answer: A) Recovery Time Objective — maximum acceptable downtime** ✅

**Explanation:**
**RTO (Recovery Time Objective)** defines the maximum amount of time a system or process can be offline after a disaster before the business impact becomes unacceptable. It answers: *"How long can we be down?"*

**RPO (Recovery Point Objective)** — how much data loss is acceptable — is frequently confused with RTO.

- **A)** — ✅ Correct definition of RTO
- **B)** — ❌ "Recovery Transfer Operation" is a fabricated term — not real
- **C)** — ❌ "Real-Time Output" is a fabricated term — not related to BCP
- **D)** — ❌ "Redundancy Testing Order" is fabricated — not a BCP/DR term

**Q20. Correct Answer: D) Bluebugging** ✅

- **Bluebugging** — Full **CONTROL** of the device — attacker can make calls, send messages, access all data

- **A) Bluejacking** — ❌ Just sends unsolicited messages — no control or data access
- **B) Bluesnarfing** — ❌ Accesses data but does not give full device control
- **C) BIAS Attack** — ❌ Exploits pairing authentication — not full device control
- **D) Bluebugging** — ✅ Full device control via Bluetooth

- **Blue Hat** — External security testers contracted by organizations

- **A) White Hat** — ❌ White hats have authorization and ethical intent
- **B) Grey Hat** — ❌ Grey hats have some moral ambiguity — not outright criminal sale of data
- **C) Blue Hat** — ❌ Blue hats are contracted testers — authorized

**Q28. Correct Answer: B) Passive Reconnaissance** ✅

**Passive Reconnaissance** means gathering information about a target WITHOUT directly interacting with or touching the target systems. WHOIS lookups, Google Dorking, and Shodan all retrieve publicly available information — the target has no way of detecting this activity.

**Active Reconnaissance** involves direct interaction — ping sweeps, port scanning, banner grabbing — which the target may detect.

- **A) Active Reconnaissance** — ❌ Requires direct interaction with target systems
- **B) Passive Reconnaissance** — ✅ No direct contact — only public sources
- **C) Hybrid Reconnaissance** — ❌ Not a standard classification term
- **D) Enumeration** — ❌ Enumeration is Phase 2 — extracting specific details from a target after scanning

- **A)** — ❌ OSINT is not a vulnerability scanner — NMAP or Nessus would be
- **B)** — ❌ Classified government sources are the OPPOSITE of "open source" — that's HUMINT or SIGINT
- **C)** — ✅ Publicly available information — correct definition of OSINT
- **D)** — ❌ MITRE ATT&CK TA0007 is "Discovery" — internal network mapping after access is gained
